PROJECT SUMMARY

I redesigned an existing website for a Chinese restaurant in Seattle. Currently, Shanghai Garden has a long scrolling single page website that is difficult to navigate, with hard-to-read menu items.

Navigation items snap down the page when clicked and it is unclear where a user is on the page after clicking a navigation item.

To give the customer a better shopping experience, the redesign creates easy to navigate pages, updated content, and an online checkout delivery option. This was done by adding additional pages to separate content, adding reviews from social media, and updating menu categories done by using a card sort. An online ordering page was added for to-go orders with an easy checkout process.

PROBLEM & GOAL

Problem

Consumers had a difficult time navigating the website as well as reading menu items. Consumers could not order online or order delivery.

The Goal

Redesign the website so that consumers will be able to navigate the site more easily and read the menu items with ease. Add additional features the persona desired for a more fun shopping experience.

Primary goals for the redesign included:
more compelling imagery a delivery and pickup order option organized menu for easily digestible content the removal of advertisements

Secondary goals are to design new pages with more in-depth information on location, about the restaurant, and a modern home page with reviews and pictures of popular food items.

RESEARCH: HOW I GOT THERE

Competitive Feature Analysis

Synthesis

There were some basic items missing from the Shanghai Garden website such as an about page, reviews of their restaurant, online ordering, and compelling pictures on their homepage. These would be added into the final design of the website.

Personas

After interviewing six participants (two consumers of Shanghai Gardens and four restaurant consumers), I came up with the following personas: Juan and Janet.


Design

Grouping themes and ideas from the personas and competitor websites, I sketched out ideas for the pages of the website. I got inspiration on designs from Imjai Thai in Portland, Din Tai Fung, King Noodle, and Green Leaf Vietnamese Restaurant.

Card Sort of Menu Categories

Since the menu seemed confusing, I created a card sort using common items. The result was the original menu items were condensed from 15 items to nine items. The new menu became more predictable and easy to navigate for the consumer.

USER TESTING

I tested six participants on navigating the website and completing a delivery order for five specific items. I asked that they give details on their feelings of using the site, anything that caught their attention, and anything that was confusing and could be updated.

Headlines were added to the home page to signify what a person was looking at and to show separation of items on the page. Social media reviews were changed to the original size from the respective websites for consistency, predictability, and visibility.

To prevent a long scrolling menu page with dozens of pictures per category, the menu items were changed from pictures to text with pricing for easy to find items.

Titles were added to the menu items and order box. The tested version gave participants the impression that the menu items in the middle were the items that were already on their order.

CONCLUSION

Participants in the user testing were pleased with the new design and had an easy time ordering items. The new design hits on key goals and needs of Juan and Janet, such as easy to read menus, take out and delivery ordering, social media information, and appealing imagery. It also assists in some pain points for Juan and Janet to be able to read the menu and order quickly.

The navigation is intuitive and illustrates the section the user has chosen on the website, while the footer provides helpful information.

To finalize the product, I would do an additional user test and collaborate with developers and other stakeholders before launching the product.
